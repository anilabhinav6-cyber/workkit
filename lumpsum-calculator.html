How lumpsum returns work

A = P × (1 + r)^t

A one-time investment compounds annually. ₹1 lakh at 12% becomes ₹3.1 lakh in 10 years, ₹9.6 lakh in 20 years, and ₹29.96 lakh in 30 years — the longer the horizon, the more dramatic the compounding curve.

Lumpsum vs SIP

Lumpsum works best when you have idle capital and a long horizon. SIP suits monthly earners and reduces market-timing risk. The math difference: a lumpsum is fully invested from day one, while SIP money enters gradually. Try our SIP calculator to compare both for your situation.

Frequently asked questions

Mathematically, lumpsum outperforms in steadily rising markets since the full amount compounds from day one. SIP wins in volatile or falling markets through cost averaging. For most salaried investors, SIP is more practical.

For Indian equity mutual funds, 10–12% is a reasonable long-term assumption. For debt funds 6–8%, for FDs use the bank's quoted rate. Conservative planning uses lower rates.
